Lab WI System — “A Day in the Life”

Reagent Production · 2-Hour Presales Demo Script

⏱ ~120 minutes 👥 Lab Scientist → Planner → Operator → Delivery → Authoring 🔗 Integrated with Dynamics 365 F&SC 🧪 Built on the Microsoft stack

This script walks one continuous story — a single day in the reagent lab — to show the whole platform without overwhelming the audience. We start small (a scientist asking for a reagent), build to the full shop-floor + quality experience, close the supply loop with a delivery, and finish in the “engine room” where the recipes are authored and governed.

The story (the supply-chain loop)

  1. Demand — A Lab Scientist requests a reagent for their bench.
  2. Plan — The production planner checks inventory & the request, then creates and schedules a production order.
  3. Make — An operator runs the batch on the floor: guided steps, scale capture, quality testing, and a released Certificate of Quality.
  4. Fulfil — A team member delivers the finished product to the requesting lab.
  5. Govern — We end in the authoring & approval workflow that produced the recipe in the first place — change control and traceability.

Why this order

Each act adds one new persona and one new slice of the app, so complexity grows gradually. By the finale the audience has seen every module and understands how the pieces connect — ending on governance leaves them with the “this is enterprise-grade and auditable” impression that wins regulated-lab business.

Pre-demo checklist (do this 30 min before)

  • Database is migrated. In the Supabase SQL editor, confirm migrations through 030 have run — especially 026 (Inventory), 027/028 (delivery + D365 start message), 029 (search name), 030 (demo scales). These power the newest screens.
  • Demo users exist incl. the Lab Scientist. Run node scripts/create-demo-users.mjs (now seeds lab@demolab.com). On the RSM proxy, prefix with $env:NODE_TLS_REJECT_UNAUTHORIZED="0".
  • Lab Scientist has a default lab. Sign in once as the Lab Scientist and pick a default lab in the sidebar (bottom). Without it, the lab role can’t see its own orders (by design — RLS scopes labs to their site).
  • One approved Work Instruction that (a) is linked to a reagent item, (b) has a Weigh step, (c) uses a lot-controlled reagent, and (d) that reagent item has QC specifications defined (Reagent Items → QC icon). This makes the scale-scan, lot capture, QC, and COQ all light up in Act 3.
  • Inventory & scales are seeded (migrations 026 & 030). Confirm a few scales show as active on the Scales page and the Inventory screen has rows.
  • A pending reagent order already in the system is a nice fallback so Act 4 has something to deliver even if Act 1 runs short.
  • Browser: zoom 110%, close other tabs. Open two windows (or two profiles) so you can keep one persona signed in while switching another — avoids re-login friction.
  • D365 toggle decision (Act 3): with D365 disabled, the “Start production order” message shows Skipped — integration disabled; that’s fine to narrate (“this is the call that posts to D365”). If you want it to show Sent, enable D365 in Reagent Items → D365 Connection Settings and set Company = USP2 beforehand.

Act 0 · Welcome & framing

Set the scene before anyone touches the screen. Keep the login page up.

“Today I’m going to walk you through one day in your reagent lab — not feature by feature, but the way work actually flows. A scientist needs a reagent. Someone plans and makes it to spec. Quality releases it. It gets delivered. And behind all of that is a governed library of recipes. You’ll see the same single application adapt to each person’s job, and you’ll see it talking to Dynamics 365 at every step. Let’s start where the work starts — with a request.”

Frame the three pains you solve

  • Paper & spreadsheets for work instructions, lot records, and QC — no audit trail.
  • Disconnected ordering between labs and the central reagent lab.
  • ERP island — production reality never makes it back into D365.
Don’t open every menu yet. Resist the “grand tour.” The power of this script is restraint — reveal each module only when the story needs it.
15 minLab Scientist — “Dana”

Act 1 · The demand signal

Goal: a satellite-lab scientist requests reagents from the central production lab — the event that kicks off everything else.

  1. On the login screen, click the blue Lab Scientist demo button.
  2. Land on the Dashboard. Note how spare the sidebar is — just Dashboard and Reagent Orders.
  3. Sidebar → Reagent Orders → New Order.
  4. Add 2–3 line items (e.g. Sodium Phosphate Dibasic — 500 g, Sodium Phosphate Monobasic — 250 g). Use the searchable item picker.
  5. Confirm the Ordering Lab (defaults to Dana’s lab), set a Needed-by date, and add a note.
  6. Tick High Priority to trigger the notification demo.
  7. Click Submit Order.
“Dana doesn’t see production orders, work instructions, scales — none of it. She sees exactly two things: her dashboard and the ability to order reagents for her bench. She builds a multi-line request, tells us when she needs it, and flags it urgent.”

Show / point out

  • The role-scoped sidebar — minimal by design for a bench user.
  • The High Priority confirmation: a mocked email + Microsoft Teams alert to the “High Priority Requests” group. Read it aloud — it personalises the order, lab, and items.
  • Back on the Reagent Orders list: the new order with a Pending status pill and (if D365 is on) a Transfer Order reference.
This is the demand signal — and it’s not a silo. The moment Dana submits, the system can raise a D365 transfer order automatically and ping the right people. No email chains, no shared spreadsheet, no “did anyone order that?”.
If D365 is disabled the transfer-order line shows “Skipped” — narrate it as “in production this fires a real D365 transfer order.” Don’t apologise for it; it’s a config toggle.
25 minAdmin / Planner — “Riley”

Act 2 · Plan & triage

Goal: the planner sees the request, checks stock and the catalog, then creates and schedules the production order. This act shows the “control tower.”

Switch persona: sign out (or use your 2nd window) → sign in as ryan@lab.com.

2.1 — Dashboard (3 min)

Land on the Dashboard. Point to the four KPI tiles and the production schedule Gantt.
“Riley runs the lab. Every morning this is the control tower — what’s approved, what’s pending review, what’s running right now, and a timeline of the whole floor’s schedule.”

2.2 — Inventory highlight (6 min)

  1. Sidebar → Inventory.
  2. Toggle the FG / RM / PKG filter pills — show how it isolates finished goods vs raw materials vs packaging.
  3. Filter by a specific reagent lab; switch the By lab / By item grouping.
  4. Read the columns: Physical, Reserved, Ordered In, On Order, Total Available.
“Before we make anything, do we even need to? This is on-hand inventory modeled exactly like the D365 Finance & Supply Chain on-hand form — physical, reserved, what’s on order, and true availability — broken down by each reagent lab, and filterable by finished good, raw material, or packaging. Notice it’s synced from D365: one version of the truth.”
The planner answers “make, buy, or transfer?” in seconds — using the same numbers Finance sees in D365. The Lab Scientist role is deliberately excluded from this screen; inventory is a planning tool, not a bench tool.

2.3 — Reagent Items catalog highlight (5 min)

  1. Sidebar → Reagent Items.
  2. Point out the Type column (FG/RM/PKG) and that Product Name is a clean display name over the raw Search Name from D365.
  3. Open one item — show CAS, molecular weight, hazard/GHS, storage, lot-controlled flag.
  4. Click the QC (clipboard) icon on a product — show its quality specifications (limits, methods).
  5. Expand D365 Connection Settings — show the live sync config incl. the MES Message Queue field.
“The catalog is the D365 item master — we don’t maintain a parallel list. On top of D365’s data we layer the lab’s world: CAS numbers, hazard pictograms, storage, lot control, and the QC panel every batch of this product is tested against. That QC panel is what drives the Certificate of Quality you’ll see later.”
Item master stays in D365; lab-specific science and quality specs live here. No duplication, and the spec that governs release is defined once, centrally.

2.4 — Triage the request & (optional) source raw materials (3 min)

  1. Sidebar → Reagent Orders. Show the multi-select status filter (defaults to Pending + In Progress) and Group by Lab / Needed By.
  2. Find Dana’s urgent request from Act 1.
  3. (Optional) Mention raw materials can be ordered/transferred the same way.
“Riley triages incoming demand — filtered to what’s actually actionable and grouped by lab or by due date. There’s Dana’s urgent phosphate request.”

2.5 — Create the production order (4 min)

  1. Sidebar → Production Orders → New Production Order.
  2. Work Instruction: pick the approved buffer recipe.
  3. Lot Number (e.g. PB-2026-0601-01), Batch Size (e.g. 2 L), Assigned to: Olivia, set a required-by date.
  4. Create.
“We turn the approved recipe into a real batch — a lot number, a size, an owner. Critically, the system stamps this order with the exact recipe version in force right now. If Alex publishes v2 tomorrow, this batch stays pinned to the version it was created against. That’s non-negotiable for traceability.”

2.6 — Schedule it (4 min)

  1. Sidebar → Unscheduled Orders.
  2. Place the new order onto a slot (e.g. tomorrow morning) on the Gantt.
  3. Return to the Dashboard to show it now on the schedule timeline.
Capacity planning and execution live in the same system as the recipe and the quality spec — so the schedule is never out of sync with what’s actually buildable.
If short on time, you can create the order and skip the Gantt drag — just point at the schedule on the Dashboard and move on.
35 minOperator — “Olivia”

Act 3 · Make the batch

Goal: the headline act. Run the batch on the floor with guided steps, live scale capture, lot tracking, quality testing, and a released Certificate of Quality. Slow down here.

Switch persona: sign in with the Operator button.

3.1 — The focused floor view (2 min)

“It’s the next morning. Olivia taps the tablet at her bench. Her world is deliberately narrow — no authoring, no admin. Just the work in front of her.”

3.2 — Open & start the order → D365 start message highlight (4 min)

  1. Sidebar → Production Orders → open the assigned batch (top of the list, Pending).
  2. Click Start Production.
  3. Watch the D365 Production Order Start card appear (Sending → Sent, or Skipped if D365 is off).
“The moment Olivia hits Start, two things happen. The app builds her step-by-step run sheet — and it posts a ProdProductionOrderStart message into the D365 message-processor framework, starting the matching production order in the ERP automatically. The floor and Finance move in lockstep.”
No double entry. Starting work on the floor starts it in D365 — the integration we wrote posts the exact production order number, company, and consumption rules to the SysMessage service.

3.3 — Work the steps (12 min) — the money shot

Walk each step deliberately. The recipe may include any of these step types — narrate the ones present:

StepWhat to show
Gather Inputs / Equipment / ReagentsA checklist the operator confirms before starting. For lot-controlled reagents, the operator must record the lot number used.
Weigh highlightThe big one — see 3.4 below.
Mix / Heat / CoolCapture actual duration / temperature against the target.
pH AdjustEnter measured pH; instant in-/out-of-tolerance check.
TransferFrom-vessel → to-vessel confirmation.
Observe / NotesFree-text observations captured into the batch record.
Print LabelsGenerates lot labels at the end of the run.

3.4 — The Weigh step: scan-to-connect scale highlight (6 min)

  1. On the Weigh step, show the target and tolerance band.
  2. In “Connect your bench scale,” scan or type a scale (e.g. SCL-A1) — or pick from the dropdown of active scales.
  3. The panel flips to a green pulsing “Connected · Live” indicator; the reading field unlocks.
  4. Enter a weight inside tolerance → green In tolerance badge.
  5. Optional drama: enter an out-of-tolerance value → red OUT OF TOLERANCE — do not proceed; then correct it.
  6. Complete the step.
“A lot of these buffers can be made at any bench, so we don’t hard-code a scale into the recipe. Instead, Olivia scans the balance in front of her — the system connects, shows a live reading, and the weight is captured against this batch with the exact scale, the measured value, the deviation, and pass/fail. If she’s out of tolerance, the system stops her. This is audit-grade capture, not a checkbox.”
We record which scale, the actual measured value, the deviation %, and the tolerance result — exactly what an investigator needs a year later. And because the operator picks the scale at run time, the same recipe works at any bench.
You’ll type the weight manually (no physical scanner needed) — the field accepts both a barcode scanner and the keyboard. The scale list comes from migration 030; have SCL-A1…SCL-M1 handy.

3.5 — Quality Control & release (5 min)

  1. Below the steps, the Quality Control card lists the product’s QC tests (from the catalog spec).
  2. Enter measured results; watch each evaluate PASS/FAIL live, with an overall All in spec badge.
  3. Click Certificate.
“Release testing happens right where the batch is made. Every result is judged against the spec we defined centrally on the item — and rolls up to a clear disposition.”

3.6 — Certificate of Quality (4 min)

  1. On the certificate page, choose Certificate of Quality (COQ) (or COA).
  2. Click Issue Certificate — a certificate number is assigned.
  3. Show the RELEASED — Conforms to specification banner, the results table, lot/version metadata, and signatures.
  4. Click Print / Save as PDF to show the print-ready document.
A regulator-ready Certificate of Quality, generated from the same data the operator captured — product, lot, batch, recipe version, every test vs spec, and who performed and released it. No re-keying into Word.

3.7 — Audit trail & trends (2 min)

Return to the completed order (all steps green, Completed, recipe-version badge). Then sidebar → Quality Trends to show QC results trending across lots.
“The batch is closed: lot stamped, recipe version stamped, every measurement and who-did-what-when captured. And across batches, the Quality Trends view turns all that captured data into process insight — drift you’d never see on paper.”
15 minTeam member

Act 4 · Close the loop — deliver to the lab

Goal: fulfil Dana’s original request — deliver the finished product to her lab with scanned from/to locations and lot capture.

Switch persona: any central-lab role (Operator/Author/Approver) can deliver. Stay as Operator or use the Author button.
  1. Sidebar → Reagent Orders.
  2. Tick the checkbox on Dana’s order (and any others going to the same lab) → Deliver Selected.
  3. On the delivery screen, note orders are grouped by destination lab — one D365 transfer order per lab.
  4. For each line, set the Deliver Qty, scan the From location (source bin at the reagent lab) and the To location (bin at the destination lab), and a LOT for lot-controlled items.
  5. Click Finalize Delivery → transfer-order numbers are generated and the orders flip to Fulfilled.
  6. Back on the list, show the delivered details on the order: qty · from → to · LOT.
“Now we close the loop. Whoever’s running deliveries selects the orders to send together, and the system batches them into transfer orders — one per destination lab. They scan where they picked it from and where it lands, capture the lot, and finalize. That’s a real warehouse transfer with bin-level, lot-level traceability — the kind of record an auditor loves.”
Emotional close: sign back in as the Lab Scientist and show Dana’s order now Fulfilled with its delivery details — the request she made in Act 1 is complete.
From a scientist’s request to a delivered, lot-traced, location-scanned product — every hop captured and reconcilable with D365. The full physical supply loop in one system.
20 minAuthor — “Alex”Approver — “Sam”

Act 5 · The engine room

Goal: reveal where the recipe that powered today comes from — authoring, the building-block step library, and the approval / version-control governance.

Switch persona: sign in with the Author button.

5.1 — The building blocks (3 min)

Sidebar → Step Library. Scroll the system step templates (Gather, Weigh, Mix, Heat, Cool, pH Adjust, Transfer, Observe, Notes, Print Labels, Custom…).
“These are the Lego bricks. Authors snap them together into a recipe — and you can add your own custom step types for procedures unique to your lab.”

5.2 — Author a Work Instruction (8 min)

  1. Sidebar → Work Instructions → New Work Instruction.
  2. Fill the header (title, product, target molarity), then add steps via the library or the + buttons.
  3. Add a Gather Inputs step (materials), then a Weigh step — point out the material auto-fills from the gathered inputs, and that no scale is chosen here: it’s selected at the bench during the run.
  4. Add Mix / pH Adjust / Observe as desired. Drag a step by its handle to reorder, then drag it back.
  5. Save Draft → then Submit for Review.
“Alex builds the recipe visually — drag-and-drop, with parameters and tolerances baked in. Notice we deliberately don’t pin a scale to the weigh step anymore; these recipes run at any bench, so the operator connects their own scale at run time. Save as a draft, then submit it for QA.”
Recipes are structured data, not documents — which is exactly why the shop floor can render them as guided steps and capture measurements against them.

5.3 — Review & approve (5 min)

  1. Switch persona: sign in with the Approver button.
  2. Dashboard → Pending Review tile (or Work Instructions filtered to Pending Review). Open Alex’s WI.
  3. Scroll the steps as the operator will see them. Optionally Reject with a comment (“add a cooling step”) to show the feedback loop, then Approve.
  4. Show the Approved badge, reviewer name, and timestamp.
“Sam sees the recipe exactly as the floor will. One click approves — or rejects with a comment that routes straight back to the author. Once approved, the recipe is frozen at this version.”

5.4 — Change control (4 min)

  1. Back as the Author (or admin), open the approved WI → New Version.
  2. Show the new draft (v2) cloned from v1, ready to edit and re-approve.
This is regulated-lab change control. v1 is preserved forever; v2 goes through its own approval. And remember Act 2 — every production order is pinned to the version it was created against, so a new version never silently changes a batch that’s already in flight.

Act 6 · Recap, ROI & Q&A

“In the last two hours we followed one reagent from request to delivery — and never left the system or re-keyed a thing. A scientist ordered it. A planner checked live D365 inventory and scheduled it. An operator made it to spec with live scale capture, tested it, and released a Certificate of Quality — while D365 started the order in the background. A teammate delivered it with bin- and lot-level scans. And we ended in the governed recipe library that makes all of it repeatable and auditable.”

Business outcomes to land

  • One role-aware app replaces paper WIs, the QA sign-off binder, the reagent order pad, the scheduler, and the floor checklist.
  • Audit-grade traceability — every batch pinned to a frozen recipe version, every measurement, lot, scale, and signature captured.
  • D365-native — inventory, item master, transfer orders, and production order start all flow through Dynamics 365. No ERP island.
  • Built on Microsoft — fits the customer’s existing stack, security, and reporting (Power BI over the D365 data).

Roadmap one-liners (for the inevitable questions)

  • 21 CFR Part 11 e-signatures — today every action is user- and time-stamped; full e-sig flow is roadmap.
  • Offline/PWA shop-floor mode — current build assumes a connected tablet.
  • Inventory auto-adjust on delivery — wiring deliveries to decrement/increment on-hand is a fast follow.

Reset between runs

  1. As Admin: Production Orders → cancel/delete the demo batch.
  2. Work Instructions → delete the v2 draft if you created one; ensure the v1 approved recipe remains.
  3. Reagent Orders → delete (or re-create) the demo order so Act 1/Act 4 start clean.
  4. Re-run create-demo-users.mjs only if passwords drifted.
